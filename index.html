Usability Testing and Needs Assessment

Working with three other classmates, I completed a full usability evaluation for CreateMyTee. I acted in each role of the process, doing everything from interviewing clients, to creating personas, to coordinating usability evaluations.


We followed a six step process to complete the assessment.

  • Interaction map
  • Interviews
  • Surveys
  • Comparative Analysis
  • Heuristic Evaluation
  • Usability Tests

This video summarizes the work we did with CreateMyTee. I narrated the video, briefly explaining each step of the process so anyone can understand what went into the analysis, then detailed the findings and recommendations we developed. I was also the main editor of the video.


The Process

We worked with CreateMyTee and their customers to find what improvements could be made to their website. We did this by conducting interviews, surveys, comparative analyses, and heuristic evaluations. We then tested our findings with usability tests to see if our findings translated to how people actually used the site. We then came up with multiple recommedations for the company, some of which they've already implemented.

Below is an example of the work I do for the University of Michigan Office of Tech Transfer.

LifeGoals Collaborative Care came to us with a request to turn their service into a mobile friendly experience for their users. Working with them, I was able to create:

  • Use Cases
  • Wireframes using Balsamiq
  • Clickable Comp in Proto.io

The clickable comp below showcases one pathway the user could use while interacting with the app. This specific comp shows the user accessing a wellness module and what it would look like, along with partial functionality of the nav bar.


Click to view the Life Goals Wireframe

The Process

The goal of our work at the University of Michigan Office of Tech Transfer is not to design an application for our clients, but instead to give them all of the tools they need in order to get sponsorship from potential investors and baseline development started.


Use Cases

I began the assignment by interviewing the client to get a background of their Life Goals, as well as to understand their needs and the needs of their patients. Next, I worked to create use cases for their potential application. We required them to conduct interviews with potential customers to better understand their needs and get some early stage input from these users. Using this data, and the notes I took during the initial interview, I created use cases for how the application will be used.


Lo-Fi Wireframes

I then worked to create wireframes for the application, confirming that all use cases are satisfied. The goal of the wireframes was not to design how the application will look, but rather to illustrate the ideas of the client and to better understand the goals of the application to maximize the utility of the customer. We use Balsamiq specifically for this reason. It looks rough, which keeps the customer from becoming too attached to how the wireframe looks, and instead focusing on functionality.


Hi-Fi Wireframes

Finally, I used the criticisms the client gave me on my wireframe to design a high-fi wireframe of their potential application. This includes attractive graphics and clickability, but doesn't include design. This is the wireframe you can view above on Proto.io.


  • Client: Life Goals Collaborative Care
  • Date: October 2015
  • Service: User Experience Design

Digital Discovery Flyer

The University of Michigan Office of Tech Transfer Department came to me in between my work with clients with a special request. They wished to have a flyer they could give to clients when they are explaining the "Digital Discovery Process". I created this flyer based on their guidelines and my understanding of what goes into the process.

The Process

I designed this flyer in order to tell a story to the potential clients. This flyer shows them what they had before the process, what they will go through during the prcoess, and what they will be ready to accomplish afterwards.


My Design

My first draft barely resembles my final draft. Over time, I started to understand what they wanted from this flyer, and that they wanted more than a simple list of objectives the client will reach. The final draft instead shows with graphics instead of explains with a list. Everything inside the arrow is what the user will go through as they work through the Digital Discovery Process with us.


What I learned

Creating this flyer was a fantastic learning experience for me. I learned, with a real client, how to creatively explain ideas and objectives in an interesting manner.


  • Client: University of Michigan Office of Tech Transfer
  • Date: July 2015

The app is a gamification of learning American culture, targeted at international students coming to the USA. It's goal is to provide comprehensive aid to students experiencing culture shock.

The theme of CHI 2015 was Crossings. Crossings included borders, cultures, technology, or anything else you could think of. We worked to cross cultures for international students in the USA.

The Concept

Beercap Fish

In the summer between my two grad school years, I decided to fill my free time with an art project instead of videogames and movies. The end result was the wooden fish you see below.


The Process

Prep Work

I began by researching the animal to use. I landed on a fish since it's easy to identify, has scales, and would be simple to cut. I then made the wood stain from sratch, by dissolving steel wool in vinegar. Finally, I decided on the wood to use. I decided to go with pine, using plywood for the body, and directional lumber for the head.

Getting Started

I began by hammering the nearly 1,000 beercaps I accumulated over the previous 5 years flat; this made them easier to attach to the body. I then bought the wood, cut it, and glued the head and tail to the body. Finally, I started gluing the caps to the body using silicon. The finishing touch was the orange eye, which I made with a soup can lid.

Videogame Mural

During the winter of my first year of grad school, I spent my down time on an art project to embody the videogames of my childhood. I then made it look professional by building a frame for it with my dad to keep it safe and looking pristine. The result was the mural you see below.


The Process

I was inspired to do this project by seeing a similar mural made out of legos on the internet. I wanted something like it, but personalized to me. I figured, who better to make it but me

Prep Work

I started by deciding on the medium to use. The legos necessary to create this would cost over $3,000, which wasn't an option for a poor grad student. I did further research and discovered perler beads, which cost less than $100 for all the materials I used and had a wide variety of colors. The last step was simply choosing the characters I would want to use, which was the definitely the most fun part.

Getting Started

The whole process was quite simple. Place the beads on a grid one by one, eventually forming the picture I wanted to make. I then took the beads off the grid with tape and repeated the process, eventually fusing them all together with an iron. The final step was building a frame, painting it, and sealing it. Simple as it is, it's also extremely time consuming, taking over 100 hours to complete.

  • Date: March 2015
